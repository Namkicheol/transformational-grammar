③ Scope of Negation
‘Scope’ here refers to the part of the meaning of a sentence that is negated.

① (32) Tom did not know my father

The addition of ‘not’ after the first auxiliary element changes the meaning of the whole sentence, so the scope of negation is the entire sentence.

② When the sentence contains a subordinate clause, the scope of negation can be all or part of the sentence.
(33) Tom didn’t say that he knew my father.
—> The scope of negation in (33) is the entire sentence because ‘not’ combines with the verb clause ‘say’.

(34) Tom said that he didn’t know my father.
—> The scope in (34) is limited to the subordinate clause because ‘not’ combines with the verb ‘know’ in  the subordinate clause.

④ Changes in the Relative Scope of Negation
(35)
a. Tom did not destroy the evidence.
b. Tom destroyed the evidence.

With adverbs like { deliberately, expressly, intentionally, knowingly, on purpose, purposely, willfully }, the scope of negation is different,
depending on whether the adverb is ‘before not’ or ‘after it’.

(36) Tom deliberately did not destroy the evidence.
(37) Tom didn’t deliberately destroy the evidence.

—> The position of the adverb causes (36) and (37) to have different meanings.
① The meaning of (36) is “Tom acted deliberately in not destroying the evidence”. — Tom did not destroy the evidence.
② The meaning of (37) os “Tom did not act deliberately in destroying the evidence” — Tom did destroy the evidence, but not on purpose.

⑤  Stress Can Focus Negation

(38)
a. Your children don’t hate school.
—> the scope of negation is the entire sentence.

b. Your children don’t hate school.
—> By stressing ‘your’, we focus the negation on that word.
—> implies that ‘there may be other children who hate school, but yours do not’

c. Your children don’t hate school.
—> implies that someone else related to you may hate school (your parents, your husband), but your children do not.

d. Your children don’t hate school.
—> implies that your children don’t actually hate school, but they might not like it’

e. Your children don’t hate school.
—> Your children do not hate school, but they do hate something else, for example, homework.

BASIC CONCEPTS : TENSE AND ASPECT

- Verb forms indicate ① the time of action expressed by the verb and ② the speaker’s view of that action in time,
(for example, as completed or ongoing, habitual or repeated.) —> Two concepts are used to describe time and
action in verbs — tense and aspect.

1. Tense
- past/ present/ future
- past와 present는 inflections on the verb로 표현되지만 future은 modals (will)이나 semi-modals (be going to)로 표현됨
- ‘yesterday’ 같은 시간 부사도 시간을 나타내줌

2. Aspect
- expresses how the speaker views the action of the verb
- for example
① an action that is seen as bounded and complete ―> perfect in aspect
② the action is seen as incomplete ―> imperfect
③ seen as repeated ―> iterative
④ seen as occurring regularly ―> habitual

- 영어에는 2가지 aspect 존재
① progressive(continuous) : ongoing action
② perfect : complete action

3. Lexical Aspect (of verbs)
: Verbs can be classified by the type of act they denote

1) Stative Verbs
● contain, hope, know, need, own, resemble, understand ―> cognitive, emotional, physical states
● express states or situations (action X)
● Chacteristics
① end point X ―> atelic verbs
② start, stop과 함께 올 수 있으나, finish와는 같이 나타나지 않음
Ex) He stopped loving Susan. *He finished loving Susan
③ How long have/has ~? 와 같은 질문 가능
Ex) How long have you known/needed/owned ~?
④ not normally occur in progressive aspect forms
Ex) *She is having a car.
⑤ not occur with most manner adverbs
Ex) *She understood methodically
⑥ ususally cannot occur in pseudocleft sentences
Ex) *What Bill did was resemble his brother.

2) Dynamic Verbs
: verbs that require some input of action by the subject ―> 3가지 종류 있음

(1) Activity verbs
● develop, grow, sit, work
● Characteristic
① the action can go on for an indefinite period of time
② atelic
- the action are continuous (observe, pull, run, sit, stare, swim) or changing (decline, develop, grow)
③ How long did ~? 로 물어보는 것 가능
Ex) How long did you work/stare at the wall? How long did it grow ?
④ can occur in the progressive aspect

(2) Achievement verbs
● describes action that occur instantaneously (He solved the problem/ She turned on the light / He spotted the airplane)
● Two classes (types)
① punctual ― bounce, faint, hit, kick
- an action that is instantaneous, ending as soon as it begins
- With punctual verbs, progressive aspect form is understood as meaning repeated (rather than ongoing) action
Ex) He is kicking the tires
② change of state ― find (a solution), cross (the finish line)
- involve preliminary activity that culminates in the act denoted by the verb
(a person ‘searches’ before finding the solution, ‘runs’ toward the finish line before crossing it)
- progressive aspect : may or may not possible, depending on whether the activity leading up to the achievement is
treated as being the same activity
Ex) His train is arriving at noon. (O)
Ex) *She is recognizing the thief. (X)

● Characteristics
① telic (have an end point)
② stop, start과 대게 함께 올 수 없음
Ex) *He stopped recognizing the thief / *He started catching the kitten
③ At what time did ~? , How long did it take to ~? 라고 물어보는 것 가능
Ex) At what time did he faint ? / How long did it take to find a solution ?

(3) Accomplishment Verbs
● attend, build, draw, make, pain, recover (from an illness), solve (achievement verbs), write
● terminate in an end point that is logical in terms of their action.
ex) He wrote a best seller ―> the action has a logical end point when the writing of the best seller is completed
● Characteristics
① telic
② actions go for a certain amount of time and end with completion ―> How long did it take to ~? 질문가능
Ex) How long did it take him to write the best seller ?
③ start, stop, finish 모두 사용 가능 ―> 하지만 stop, finish 의미차이존재
- If the action is stopped, the accomplishment does not occur
④ With accomplishment verbs, the subject performs the action of the verb ‘in’ a certain amount of time, not ‘for’ a certain
amount of time
Ex) They built the stadium in less than a year. (*They built the stadium for less than a year)

NONRESTRICTIVE RELATIVE CLAUSES

Form Criteria Distinguishing Nonrestrictive and Restrictive Relative Clauses

1) punctuation
- Restrictive relative clauses must not be separated by commas.
(69)
a. My sister, who lives in Canada, is a biologist.
b. My sister who lives in Canada is a biologist.

2) Intonation
- Nonrestrictive relative clauses are marked by pauses and by a falling intonation pattern at the end of the clause.
- Restrictive relative clauses do not have this epcial intonation pattern.

3) modiﬁcation of proper nouns
- nonrestrictive relative clauses can modify proper nouns, whereas restrictive relatives cannot.
(71)
a. John, who is a linguist, was not impressed by Professor Fish’s arguments.
b. *John who is a linguist was not impressed by Professor Fish’s arguments.

4) modiﬁcation of ‘any, every, no’
- nonrestrictive relative clauses may not modify [any, every, or no + noun] or indeﬁnite pronouns such as ‘anyone, everyone, no one’
(72)
a. *Any man, who goes back on his word, is no friend of mine.
b. Any man who goes back on his word is no friend of mine.

5) ‘That’ as relative pronoun
- Nonrestrictive relative clauses may not be introduced by ‘that’; restrictive relatives may.
(73)
a. *The plan, that we discussed yesterday, will be adopted.
b. The plan that we discussed yesterday will be adopted.

6) Stacking
- nonrestrictive relative clauses cannot be stacked.
(74)
a. *They gave the job to Rob, who is very qualiﬁed, who starts  next month.
b. They really like that car that you have that your wife is always zipping around town in.

7) sentence modiﬁcation
- nonrestrictive relative clauses may modify an entire sentence, that is, a preceding independent clause.
- restrictive relatives may only modify noun phrases.
(75)
a. Professor Fish gave everyone an A, which was just ﬁne with Alice.
b. *Professor Fish gave everyone an A which was just ﬁne with Alice.
